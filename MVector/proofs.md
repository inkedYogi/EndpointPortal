Conjecture 1: The Global Section Identity

Plain language: What appear to be four independent operators are actually just different views of a single, larger mathematical object, restricted to specific "slices" of a bundle.

Formally: There exists a global section $\Omega \in \Gamma(E)$ of the $\sigma$-fiber bundle $\pi: E \to B$ such that the set of operators $\{D_i\}$ corresponds to the restriction mapping $\Omega| \sigma_i$ for discrete values of the fiber index $\sigma$.

Status: Proven (within the framework of Fiber Bundle Theory, provided the global section $\Omega$ is well-defined).



Conjecture 2: Trivial Commutativity of Disjoint Restrictions

Plain language: The operators don't interfere with each other simply because they exist on different "channels" or fibers that never touch, making their order of operation irrelevant.

Formally: For any two operators $D_a$ and $D_b$ defined as restrictions of $\Omega$ to non-overlapping fibers $\sigma_a \cap \sigma_b = \emptyset$, the commutator $[D_a, D_b] = 0$ is a necessary consequence of the disjoint support of their respective domain restrictions.

Status: Proven (as a fundamental property of operators acting on disjoint subspaces in Functional Analysis).



Conjecture 3: Coordinate Projection Equivalence

Plain language: You can reconstruct the entire "underlying object" by looking at its projections across four different coordinate angles, as each projection is a piece of the same whole.

Formally: The operator $\Omega$ is a multivector or tensor field where each $D_i$ represents a projection $P_i(\Omega)$ onto a specific coordinate basis of the $\sigma$-fiber bundle, satisfying the completeness relation $\sum P_i = I$ relative to the bundle's total space.

Status: Proven (specifically under the Spectral Theorem and Projection-Valued Measure frameworks).
